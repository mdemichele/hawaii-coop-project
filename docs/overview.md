# Hawaii Co-op Project — Product Overview

## Vision

Hawaii Co-op Project is a web platform that helps communities in Hawaii form and operate cooperative organizations. It focuses on the two most impactful co-op types for Hawaii's unique socioeconomic context: housing co-ops (addressing the affordability crisis) and agricultural co-ops (supporting local food production and land stewardship).

The platform guides a group through every stage of the co-op lifecycle — from initial organizing, through legal formation, into ongoing operations — removing the expertise barrier that prevents most grassroots efforts from succeeding.

---

## Why Co-ops?

Western capitalism divides people into two categories: those who own and those who work for those who own. This division is not incidental — it is structural. Capital accumulates toward those who already have it, while the people who actually build, grow, harvest, and maintain things see little of the wealth they create. Most of us spend our working lives inside organizations we have no real stake in, generating value for owners who may never set foot in our neighborhoods and have no particular reason to care whether our communities thrive.

Ownership changes behavior. Anyone who has ever owned something — a home, a small business, a piece of land — knows that you tend it differently than something that belongs to someone else. You invest in it. You protect it. You think about its future. When ownership is concentrated in distant hands, that attentiveness disappears from local communities. Decisions get made by people with no skin in the local game, and communities bear the consequences.

Co-ops are a direct response to this problem. A cooperative is an organization owned and governed by the people who use it — the workers, the residents, the farmers. Every member holds real equity. Every member has a voice in how the organization is run. Profit and benefit flow back to the people who created them, not to external shareholders. The organization is accountable to its community because its community is its ownership.

This is not a radical or untested idea. There are thousands of successful co-ops operating across the United States and around the world — grocery stores, credit unions, housing complexes, farms, utilities. What is rare is accessible infrastructure that makes it easy for ordinary people to form one. That is what this platform is for.

## Problem

Forming a co-op in Hawaii is hard:

- **Legal complexity.** Hawaii has specific statutory requirements under HRS Chapter 421 (cooperatives) and Chapter 421H (housing cooperatives). Most organizers don't know these exist.
- **Document burden.** Articles of incorporation, bylaws, operating agreements, membership agreements — each requires legal fluency most organizers lack.
- **Governance gaps.** Without clear decision-making structures, young co-ops fragment. Many dissolve within two years.
- **Financial opacity.** Tracking member equity, dues, and operating expenses across a shared organization is genuinely difficult without purpose-built tooling.
- **No local hub.** There is no central resource connecting co-op organizers, advisors, and service providers in Hawaii.

## Target Users

| User | Description |
|---|---|
| **Organizer** | A person or small group that wants to start a co-op. May have no legal or business background. Needs guided, step-by-step support. |
| **Co-op Member** | A person who has joined a formed co-op. Needs to participate in governance, see financials, and access shared documents. |
| **Co-op Administrator** | A member who manages the co-op's day-to-day: approving members, tracking finances, running votes. |
| **Advisor / Supporter** | A lawyer, accountant, USDA agent, or community organization that supports multiple co-ops. Needs visibility into several organizations. |

## Scope (v1)

Hawaii Co-op Project v1 covers:

1. **Formation** — Guided workflow to legally form a housing or agricultural co-op in Hawaii.
2. **Membership** — Member registry, equity tracking, and onboarding.
3. **Governance** — Proposal and voting system aligned with co-op democratic principles.
4. **Documents** — Secure storage for governing documents with Hawaii-specific templates.
5. **Meetings** — Agenda management and minute recording.

Out of scope for v1: payment processing, land title management, loan origination, public marketplace/directory.

---

## Hawaii-Specific Context

- **Land tenure.** Much of Hawaii's land is held under leasehold. Housing co-ops must navigate DHHL (Department of Hawaiian Home Lands) rules for Native Hawaiian beneficiaries, and standard fee-simple vs. leasehold structures for others.
- **Agricultural land.** The state has active programs (ALISH, Beginning Farmer programs) that agricultural co-ops can access. The platform should surface these during formation.
- **Cultural norms.** The "ohana" (family/community) model of shared responsibility resonates deeply. The platform's UX should reflect this ethos — collective language, not corporate.
- **Island-by-island variation.** Regulations and land availability differ across Oahu, Maui, Hawaii Island, Kauai, and Molokai. The platform must accommodate multi-island contexts.

## Success Metrics (v1)

- Co-ops successfully formed through the platform: **10 in year 1**
- Median time to complete formation workflow: **< 6 weeks**
- Member retention (still active co-op at 12 months): **> 70%**
- NPS from organizers completing formation: **> 50**
